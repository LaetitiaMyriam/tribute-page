André Kertesz

A star as a photographer

André Kertesz circa 1980, by Bill Rauhauser, © Bill Rauhauser 2010

Here is a time line of André Kertesz's life

  • 1894 - Born in Budapest in Hungary
  • 1912 - Buys his first camera
  • 1917 - His photographs are first published in the magazine Érdekes Újság
  • 1920 - Works as a beekeeper and agricultor
  • 1923 - The Hungarian Amateur Photographer's Association selects one of his photographs for its silver award, he turns down the medal but is given a diploma
  • 1925 - Emigrates to Paris
  • 1927 - First photographer to have a one-man exhibition : Jan Slivinsky presented 30 of his photographs at the "Sacre du Printemps Gallery"
  • - Soon after his arrival, Kertész approached Beaumont Newhall, director of the photographic department at the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA)
  • - Soon after his arrival, Kertész approached Beaumont Newhall, director of the photographic department at the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA)
  • 1936 - emigrates to New York whith his wife Elizabeth, where within a decade, they become naturalized citizens. Soon after his arrival, Kertész approaches Beaumont Newhall, director of the photographic department at the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA)
  • 1937 - Kertész has his first solo show in New York at the PM Gallery
  • 1938 - Look printed a series of Kertész photographs, entitled A Fireman Goes to School; but credited them mistakenly to Ernie Prince, his former boss. Infuriated, Kertész considered never working with photo magazines again.
  • 1941 - The Kertész couple are designated as enemy aliens because of World War II
  • 1944 - become US citizens. László Moholy-Nagy, director of the New Bauhaus - American School of Design offered him a position teaching photography. Despite the honor, he turns the offer down
  • 1946 - Solo exhibition at the Art Institute of Chicago, featuring photographs from his Day of Paris series
  • 1963 -One of the invited artists of the IV Mostra Biennale Internazionale della Fotografia there and he is awarded a gold medal for his dedication to the photographic industry. His work is shown in Paris at the Bibliothèque nationale de France
  • 1964 - Soon after John Szarkowski becomes the photography director at the Museum of Modern Art, he features Kertész in a solo show
  • 1979 - The Polaroid Corporation gives him one of their new SX-70 cameras, which he experimentes with into the 1980s. Still growing in fame
  • 1985 - Dies at the age of 90
